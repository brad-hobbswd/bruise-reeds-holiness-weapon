Chapter Seven

Lies Among the Saints

Some of the deepest wounds within the Church are created not by open opposition but by words spoken quietly in trusted places. They are often wrapped in concern, supported with spiritual language, and shared under the appearance of wisdom. Yet beneath them is something far different. They divide instead of healing. They protect reputations rather than people. They create fear where Christ intended peace.

Those who have been the subject of these conversations rarely forget them. They remember learning that discussions had taken place without their knowledge. They remember the changed expressions, the distant conversations, the opportunities that quietly disappeared, and the realization that others seemed to know a story they had never been asked to tell themselves.

Rumors rarely remain unchanged. As they pass from one person to another, details are added, motives are assumed, and uncertainty slowly transforms into confidence. What began as speculation soon sounds like certainty. Before long, assumptions carry greater influence than truth, and the individual at the center of the conversation becomes increasingly isolated without ever understanding why.

The greatest tragedy is that these conversations often occur among people who sincerely desire to honor God. Yet good intentions cannot redeem harmful actions. Truth never requires secrecy to remain true. Genuine love refuses to build conclusions upon conversations that exclude the very person being discussed.

Sometimes ministries become more important than the people they were created to serve. Protecting an organization's image begins to outweigh protecting the dignity of individuals. Concern quietly becomes surveillance. Spiritual authority becomes control. The desire to preserve reputation slowly overshadows the command to love one another as Christ has loved us.

Jesus demonstrated another way entirely. He never relied upon rumor to determine someone's worth. He called people by name, invited them into conversation, listened before responding, and restored dignity before offering correction. His ministry consistently reflected openness, compassion, and truth spoken face to face rather than whispered from a distance.

The Church is called to reflect that same character. Gossip cannot be overcome by silence alone. It requires honesty, humility, accountability, and the courage to interrupt harmful conversations before they continue. Healing begins when believers refuse to repeat what they have not personally witnessed and instead encourage direct, loving conversation grounded in truth.

This chapter exists for those who have carried the pain of being misunderstood through words they never had the opportunity to answer. It is also an invitation for every believer to examine how easily conversations can drift away from grace and toward judgment. Every follower of Christ is called to become a guardian of truth rather than a carrier of rumor.

Bruised reeds remember the wounds left by careless words, yet they also remind us that restoration remains possible. When truth replaces rumor, when repentance replaces pride, and when love speaks louder than suspicion, healing begins. The Church most faithfully reflects Jesus when every conversation honors both truth and the dignity of the people He came to redeem.
