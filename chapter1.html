Chapter One

The Closet Built by the Church

Closets are not always built with wood and hinges. Some are built with silence. Others are built through fearful glances, whispered conversations, unanswered questions, and assumptions that are allowed to grow without truth. Some are built through prayer requests offered without permission or concerns expressed without ever inviting a person into the conversation.

Many people never entered these closets because of who they were. They entered because they slowly learned it was safer to hide than to be fully known. Long before doctrine was discussed or beliefs were questioned, they sensed something had changed. The atmosphere shifted. Conversations became guarded. Their presence was evaluated rather than welcomed. No one explained the rules, yet somehow everyone understood them.

They learned which words should remain unspoken. They discovered which friendships were safer to keep private and which parts of their lives created discomfort in others. Over time, silence became a survival strategy. The closet was never built in a single day. It was constructed piece by piece through fear, misunderstanding, and the quiet expectation that honesty carried too great a cost.

Often this was described as wisdom, discretion, maturity, or protecting the testimony of the Church. Yet beneath those explanations was something very different. Too often what was protected was comfort rather than compassion. People found themselves judged without conversation, categorized without consent, and misunderstood without ever being asked to share their own story.

Sometimes concern arrived disguised as kindness. Someone would quietly say they simply wanted to check on you because they had noticed something. Yet when asked what they had noticed, no clear answer followed. The purpose was rarely understanding. The purpose had already become confirmation. Once curiosity gives way to suspicion, the closet has already begun to take shape.

Silence can become one of the most powerful forms of exclusion. It leaves no obvious evidence. There are no difficult conversations, no direct accusations, and often no explanations. Instead, invitations become fewer, relationships grow distant, and conversations gradually disappear. Nothing has officially happened, yet everything feels different. Decisions have already been made without your voice ever being heard.

Holiness separated from genuine relationship becomes fragile. Holiness that refuses honest questions eventually becomes fearful. Holiness that depends upon secrecy ceases to reflect the character of Jesus. Throughout His ministry, Jesus continually invited people into the light. He listened before correcting, welcomed before judging, and restored before condemning. He never required people to hide in order to follow Him.

Many believers eventually learn to edit themselves simply to feel safe. They smile while quietly carrying fear. They worship while searching for exits. They serve while wondering whether they truly belong. This is not transformation. It is survival. Closets do not strengthen faith. They slowly fracture it. Prayer becomes difficult. Trust becomes fragile. Community becomes exhausting. Some remain but quietly shrink. Others leave carrying wounds few people ever recognize.

Jesus never confused fear with obedience. If God is one and His name is Jesus, then following Him does not require hiding. It calls us into truth held together with mercy. The Church was never intended to build closets. It was called to become a place where people could honestly say, "You do not have to hide here." Bruised reeds are not restored in darkness. They begin healing when they are finally welcomed into the light of Christ.
